Idaho has more soakable hot springs than any other state, and a surprising number of them are within an easy drive of Cascade. Some are developed, with changing rooms and a gate you pay at. Others are little more than a rock pool at the edge of a river, reached by a short scramble down a forest road. Both kinds are worth your time. Here's a local's guide to the ones we send guests to most, and how to enjoy them without getting in over your head.

## Gold Fork Hot Springs: The Easy One

If you want a hot spring you can count on, this is it. Gold Fork sits between Cascade and Donnelly, about a 30-minute drive north of the lodge, with the last few miles on gravel. It's privately run and well cared for, with six natural rock pools stair-stepping down the hillside.

- The pools range from around 85°F to a toasty 110°F, so you can move up or down until you find your temperature
- There's a cash-only day-use fee, so bring bills. No cards accepted
- Changing rooms, restrooms, and swimsuit rentals are on-site
- It's open year-round but typically closed on Tuesdays and doesn't open until midday, so check hours before you drive out

Because it's tended and family-friendly, Gold Fork is the pick if you're bringing kids or you just want a hot soak without a wilderness adventure attached.

## Trail Creek Hot Springs: The Wild One

Trail Creek, which you'll also see called Samuel's, is the opposite experience, and a lot of people's favorite for exactly that reason. It's about 19 miles east of Cascade on Warm Lake Road, followed by a short, steep walk down from the pullout to the river.

At the bottom you'll find rock-and-sand pools built right beside the creek, with 360-degree mountain views and no fee, no gate, and no facilities. Hot water flows in near boiling and mixes with the cold creek, so the temperature shifts depending on how the pools are dammed that day. It's primitive, it's free, and on a clear night the stars are unreal.

Come prepared: pack a headlamp, water shoes for the rocky bottom, and a trash bag to carry out everything you bring in.

## A Few More Worth Knowing

Once you've done the big two, the area keeps giving. Molly's Hot Springs and Molly's Tubs are a cluster of primitive roadside soaks along the North Fork of the Payette, and there are several other undeveloped springs scattered down the forest roads toward Warm Lake and beyond.

The catch with wild springs is that they change. River levels, snowmelt, and the occasional flood can bury a pool for a season or wash out a soaking spot entirely. Ask us at the front desk what's soaking well before you head out; we usually have the current word.

## How to Soak Like a Local

A few unwritten rules keep these places good for everyone:

- **Test before you get in.** Source water can be scalding. Ease in a hand or foot first, especially at primitive springs.
- **Pack it in, pack it out.** Developed springs have trash cans; wild ones don't. Leave nothing behind, including cans and bottles.
- **Skip the soap and glass.** Both ruin a natural pool for the next group.
- **Mind spring runoff.** In late spring and early summer, high, fast water can submerge or wash out riverside pools. If the creek is roaring, save the wild springs for later in the season and stick to Gold Fork.
- **Go early or late.** Weekend afternoons draw crowds. Mornings and weeknights are quieter and, at the primitive spots, better for stargazing.

## When to Go

There's no bad season for a soak. Fall and winter are magic: steam rising off the pools while snow sits on the pines is the whole reason people fall in love with Idaho hot springs. Summer works too, especially in the cool of the morning or after a day on the lake. The one window to watch is peak spring runoff, when the wild riverside springs can be underwater or unsafe. That's the time to choose the developed pools at Gold Fork.
